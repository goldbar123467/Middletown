# Middletown Gameplay Story

Middletown is a mid-sized Midwestern town of 100,000 people. It has a river, a courthouse square, a medical campus, older factory neighborhoods, a community college, logistics warehouses, subdivisions, a tired mall, and a downtown that is never as dead or as saved as people claim.

The player is the newly elected mayor. They did not inherit a throne. They inherited a calendar, a budget, staff vacancies, public records requests, aging pipes, contract negotiations, traffic complaints, budget hearings, and a town full of people who are not wrong to be impatient.

## Core Promise

The game asks whether a local government can remain competent, trusted, solvent, and humane under ordinary pressure. There are no monsters and no magic. The danger is drift: deferred maintenance, cynical meetings, slow services, angry headlines, tired staff, and neighborhoods deciding City Hall is not for them.

## Opening

On inauguration morning, the mayor walks from the civic plaza into City Hall while crews salt the steps. The outgoing administration left a balanced budget on paper, but three problems are already waiting: a water main break near Northside Elementary, a downtown redevelopment vote, and a police staffing hearing that has every council member nervous.

The chief of staff says the first rule of Middletown is simple: every choice has a room full of people who can explain why you are moving too fast and another room full of people who can explain why you are already late.

## Four Acts

Act 1, The Ledger Opens: The player learns the budget, departments, districts, and service pressures. Early choices are visible but survivable.

Act 2, The Town Pushes Back: Deferred maintenance, labor negotiations, housing fights, and business anxiety start interacting.

Act 3, The Hard Winter: A storm, revenue dip, infrastructure failure, or trust crisis forces the mayor to decide what kind of government Middletown will be when capacity is thin.

Act 4, The Public Record: The final year measures what people remember. Victory is not perfection. Victory is a town that still has options, trust, working services, and enough shared story to keep solving problems.

## Main Characters

- Elena Marquez, Chief of Staff: precise, loyal, allergic to vague promises.
- Thomas Reed, Finance Director: knows the fund balance by heart and fears structural deficits more than bad headlines.
- Arjun Patel, Public Works Director: patient, blunt, and tired of ribbon cuttings for projects his crews kept alive for years.
- Denise Walker, Council President: believes process is how a town argues without breaking.
- Ruth Kim, Library Director: sees every policy through the people who need warmth, Wi-Fi, forms, and a human desk.
- Marcus Bell, Police Chief: wants staffing, legitimacy, and fewer public safety debates that pretend those goals are opposites.
- Priya Shah, Planning Director: knows every zoning reform creates winners, losers, and three meetings about parking.

## How The Story Plays

Each quarter, the player sets priorities, funds departments, starts projects, and makes civic actions. The engine then resolves service quality, budget stress, public trust, department morale, neighborhood equity, infrastructure condition, and news pressure. Council and news events create decision points, and the chronicle records what the town will remember.

## Civic Throughlines

Middletown should feel like one town with recurring arguments, not a random
generator of municipal nouns. The data packs should keep returning to these
throughlines from different angles:

- Northside: aging mains, school crossings, rental repairs, and the feeling
  that City Hall notices the neighborhood only after a near-miss.
- East Bank: flood risk, riverfront access, lift stations, health impacts, and
  development pressure that cannot be separated from water.
- Southgate: mall redevelopment, shift-work mobility, subdivisions, retail
  corridors, and families trying to reach school, clinics, and jobs.
- Westhaven: clinics, senior housing, heat risk, winter readiness, and the
  difference between medical-campus growth and neighborhood stability.
- Midtown: renters, small shops, the library, construction disruption, and the
  need for plain schedules residents can trust.
- University District: student rentals, community college access, late-night
  safety, public desks, and education as a city-service question.
- Airport Industrial: freight, utility burden, worker housing, shift schedules,
  and whether growth includes the people moving the goods.
- Downtown: the courthouse square, budget hearings, transit transfers, civic
  legitimacy, storefronts, and the public record of the term.

The main cast should appear as civic operators with different kinds of
authority. Elena Marquez frames choices, Thomas Reed protects the ledger, Arjun
Patel names the deferred maintenance, Denise Walker defends public process,
Ruth Kim sees access from the front desk, Marcus Bell connects safety to
legitimacy, and Priya Shah turns growth fights into maps and hearings.

## Content Schema Notes

Data files can be large, but each entry should earn its place by doing a clear
job:

- Civic actions are agenda moves. Their labels should sound like verbs the mayor
  can put on a public calendar.
- Council events are public arguments. Their options should preserve real
  tradeoffs between speed, legitimacy, cost, capacity, and delay.
- News events are pressure from outside City Hall. They can reuse the same
  underlying issue, but the framing should sound like coverage, not another
  committee packet.
- Residents are memory anchors. Their quotes should name a lived detail from a
  district, not only a metric.
- Projects are visible capital promises. Their descriptions should mention the
  place, the physical work, and the civic risk if the project is oversold.
- Policies are standing rules. Their names should sound adoptable by council and
  their descriptions should explain the recurring behavior they change.
- Story samples are connective tissue. They should echo actions, events,
  residents, and endings so a sixth grader can see how one local problem becomes
  a budget request, a headline, a resident memory, and a consequence.

When a playable file uses a smaller curated set and an archive preserves the
larger generated pack, the curated set should carry the strongest opening
experience while the archive keeps the project from losing content volume.

## Endings

- The Trusted Mayor: trust and services stay high while debt remains controlled.
- The Builder: infrastructure and housing improve enough to change the town's trajectory.
- The Stabilizer: no one calls the term inspiring, but Middletown is solvent, staffed, and calmer.
- The Boomtown Mayor: business growth is strong, but the ending asks who benefited.
- The Crisis Mayor: the town survives emergencies but loses civic patience.
- The Hollow Victory: numbers look fine while trust collapses.
- The Recall Winter: services, trust, and budget fail together.

## Design Rule

Middletown should feel like The Lord's Ledger in structure but not in costume. The engine is a civic pressure machine. The fantasy is not power. The fantasy is competence.
